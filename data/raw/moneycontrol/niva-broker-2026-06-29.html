After decades of delay, Bengaluru suburban rail may finally roll in 2028

Bengaluru's long-pending suburban rail project was first proposed in 1983, received the Centre's approval in 2020, and saw Prime Minister Narendra Modi lay the foundation stone in June 2022. The 148-km network comprises four corridors. While the original target was to complete the entire project by 2026, not a single corridor has become operational so far.

The project gathered pace after Lakshman Singh, a 1996-batch Indian Railway Service of Engineers (IRSE) officer, took charge as the full-time Managing Director of Rail Infrastructure Development Company (Karnataka) Ltd (K-RIDE) in December 2025. In an interview with Moneycontrol, Singh spoke about the project's progress, revised timelines, execution challenges and long-term vision.

Edited excerpts:

Q: What is the current status of the Bengaluru suburban rail project?

The project is progressing well. Land acquisition for the main alignments and stations of Corridors 2 and 4 has been completed, with the remaining acquisition largely related to flyovers and road overbridges.

For the 46-km Corridor 4, two of the four civil packages have been tendered, while the remaining two will be floated by the middle of next month.

For 27-km Corridor 2, two of the three civil packages have been awarded. The third package has been retendered, with bids expected to open in early July. Construction has commenced at all stations on both corridors.

Q: What is the status of Corridors 1 and 3?

Indian Railways has approved alignments for Corridor 1 (41.Km Sampige Line connecting KSR Bengaluru to Devanahalli via Kempegowda International Airport) and Corridor 3 (35.5 km Parijatha Line linking Kengeri and Whitefield via KSR Bengaluru). Joint measurements are now underway to identify the exact land requirement and survey numbers. Once this exercise is completed, land acquisition under the Karnataka Industrial Areas Development (KIAD) Act will begin. Since the statutory notification process itself takes nearly a year, we expect to secure most of the required land by the end of this year or early next year.

Q: The project has missed several deadlines. Why has execution taken so long?

Large railway infrastructure projects inevitably take time. Even Bengaluru Metro has taken nearly two decades to reach its current stage. We are executing nearly 70km across Corridors 2 and 4 simultaneously.

For comparison, Bengaluru Metro has commissioned around 96km over several years. We have already taken up a comparable volume of work at once.

Q: But isn't suburban rail easier to build than Metro since much of it runs at grade?

Not really. In many ways, suburban rail is more challenging than Metro because much of the work is carried out alongside active railway tracks, where space and access are limited. Unlike Metro construction along roads, we cannot work simultaneously at multiple locations while passenger and freight trains continue to operate. This requires much stricter safety standards. Even at-grade sections involve extensive earthwork, formation strengthening and frequent transitions between elevated and ground-level sections.

Q: Corridor 3 (Kengeri-Whitefield) appears to be progressing much slower. Has it been scrapped?

We are implementing the approved DPR in phases. Our immediate focus is on Corridors 2 and 4, followed by Corridor 1. Within Corridor 3, we have prioritised the Bengaluru Cantonment-Kengeri section, while the Bengaluru Cantonment-Whitefield stretch will be taken up later. South Western Railway's quadrupling work on the Bengaluru Cantonment-Whitefield section will help meet local train requirements.

Q: Will K-RIDE operate suburban trains on the Bengaluru Cantonment-Whitefield section once the quadrupling work is completed?

It's too early to take that call.  Our signalling system will be different from South Western Railway's. The utilisation of the additional railway infrastructure will depend on South Western Railway's operational plans. As far as K-RIDE is concerned, Corridor 3 remains part of our long-term plans.

Q: When can commuters expect the first suburban train to begin operations?

We have committed to starting services by December 2028, and we are working towards that target. Our aim is to operationalise the entire Corridor 2 by then. If that is not possible, we will commission a priority section first.

Corridor 2 has been divided into three construction packages to enable phased commissioning. If Chikkabanavara-Yeshwantpur priority section is completed first, services can begin on that stretch. Yeshwanthpur will serve as a major multimodal hub integrating suburban rail, Metro, Indian Railways and BMTC services.

Q: How will suburban rail transform Bengaluru?

Unlike Metro, which largely follows major roads, suburban rail runs along existing railway corridors that pass through relatively underdeveloped parts of the city. This will create opportunities for transit-oriented development, multimodal hubs and new growth corridors, reshaping Bengaluru's urban development over the long term.

Many of these railway corridors pass through low-density areas dominated by one- and two-storey buildings. As suburban rail develops, these areas will witness significant urban transformation.

Q: Many say the network does not extend far enough into Bengaluru's expanding suburbs. Is K-RIDE planning future regional connectivity?

The state government is examining regional rapid transit proposals similar to Delhi-Meerut Regional Rapid Transit System (RRTS), and K-RIDE could play a role if such projects are approved. Regional rapid transit is a natural extension of suburban rail, and K-RIDE is well placed to execute such projects.

We are also considering adopting European Train Control System (ETCS) signalling system across all corridors. ETCS is better suited for higher-speed regional operations than CBTC and will make the network future-ready if it expands beyond Bengaluru.
